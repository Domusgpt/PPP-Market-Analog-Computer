## 1. Three Starting Propositions

This paper proceeds from three propositions, minimal in number but far-reaching in consequence. Each addresses unresolved problems in contemporary physics and philosophy of mind; together they constitute a unified framework from which the subsequent analysis derives.

### 1.1 Information Constitutes the Fundamental Substrate

The irreducible constituent of reality is information, understood not as description but as ontological primitive. Physical quantities—mass, charge, spacetime geometry—emerge as patterns in underlying information structure. Interactions are information exchanges; what appears as "matter" and "energy" represents stable configurations of informational degrees of freedom.

This proposition addresses the persistent failure to unify general relativity and quantum mechanics. Both theories presuppose incompatible ontologies: GR treats spacetime as smooth geometric continuum while QM treats matter as discrete probabilistic system. Decades of attempted unification have produced mathematical elaboration but no falsifiable predictions. The proposition resolves this by treating both spacetime geometry and quantum states as emergent from a common informational substrate—an approach consonant with Wheeler's "it from bit" hypothesis, the holographic principle's encoding of bulk physics on boundary information, and recent work on emergent spacetime from quantum entanglement (Van Raamsdonk, 2010; Maldacena & Susskind, 2013).

The proposition has immediate empirical content. If information is fundamental, then the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy S = A/4l_P² represents not merely missing information about black hole microstates but actual information content—one bit per Planck area of horizon. The holographic bound becomes a statement about reality's storage capacity rather than our epistemic limitations.

### 1.2 Dual Entropic Drives Govern All Dynamics

The dynamics of every system are governed by two complementary tendencies: a dispersive drive toward maximum entropy and uniform distribution, and an integrative drive toward increased information integration and organized complexity. These drives operate universally, at all scales, concurrently.

The dispersive drive is the Second Law of Thermodynamics: isolated systems tend toward maximum entropy. The integrative drive is more novel: it asserts that information entropy tends to decrease wherever energy gradients permit—that systems spontaneously form patterns, correlations, and structures that increase integrated information content.

This proposition addresses the "why now?" problem of cosmology: why does the universe contain structure at all, given the Second Law's prediction of heat death? Standard physics treats structure as statistical fluctuation—temporary local order paid for by greater environmental entropy. This proposition elevates structure-formation to fundamental status: the integrative drive creates galaxies, stars, life, and mind not as accidents but as expressions of a deep tendency toward information integration.

The proposition connects to recent work on infodynamics. Vopson (2019, 2022) proposes a "second law of infodynamics" asserting that information entropy decreases over time—precisely the integrative drive stated in information-theoretic language. England's (2013) dissipation-driven adaptation demonstrates that systems under energy flux self-organize to maximize entropy production—structure arising because it accelerates overall entropic flow. These are not separate phenomena but aspects of the same duality.

### 1.3 The Universe Is Self-Contained and Self-Referential

The universe constitutes a closed system with no external environment. Initial conditions, boundary conditions, and the regularities we call "physical laws" all emerge from internal consistency requirements rather than external imposition. Observers are participants in the system being observed, not external witnesses.

This proposition addresses several otherwise intractable problems. The cosmological constant puzzle—why vacuum energy density is 120 orders of magnitude smaller than quantum field theory predicts—cannot be solved by appeal to external cancellation mechanisms or anthropic selection. A self-contained universe must regulate its vacuum energy internally; the observed small positive value represents residual after structure formation has absorbed available information capacity.

The proposition also grounds the measurement problem in quantum mechanics. If observation is the universe processing information about itself, then the transition from superposition to definite outcome is not mysterious external intervention but self-consistent internal record-keeping. This resonates with Wheeler's participatory universe, Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics, and the QBist interpretation.

## 2. Convexity as Mathematical Necessity

Given the proposition of dual entropic drives, a question arises: when opposing forces—integrative and dispersive, attraction and repulsion, entropy-maximizing and pattern-forming—reach stable balance, what geometric form does that equilibrium take?

This section establishes that the answer is not contingent but necessary: **convex structure emerges wherever equilibrium obtains because the mathematics of equilibrium requires convexity.**

### 2.1 Thermodynamic Stability Requires Convex Energy Surfaces

The fundamental theorem of thermodynamic stability states: a system is stable against small perturbations if and only if its energy function U(S,V,N,...) is convex in extensive variables. Equivalently, the entropy function S(U,V,N,...) must be concave.

The mathematical content is decisive. If U were not convex, small energy fluctuations would be self-amplifying rather than self-correcting; the system would fragment into phases with no stable equilibrium. The requirement that ∂²U/∂S² > 0 ensures that temperature increases with entropy, guaranteeing heat flows from hot to cold. The requirement that ∂²U/∂V² > 0 ensures positive compressibility, guaranteeing pressure responds correctly to volume changes.

The Legendre transform f*(y) = sup_x{⟨x,y⟩ - f(x)} bridges entropy and free energy. If f is convex, f* is convex; if f is concave, f* is convex in the transformed variable. Thermodynamic potentials form convex-concave dual pairs—entropy S(E) concave, Helmholtz free energy F(T) convex—connected by Legendre transformation. This duality is not optional; it is the mathematical requirement for consistent thermodynamics.

Maxwell relations emerge from equality of mixed partial derivatives of convex potentials. The entire structure of classical thermodynamics—phase equilibria, response functions, stability criteria—rests on convexity. A universe governed by entropy maximization necessarily produces convex energy landscapes.

### 2.2 Strategic Equilibrium Requires Convex Strategy Spaces

The Debreu-Glicksberg-Fan theorem establishes: in a game where each player's strategy space S_i is non-empty, compact, and convex, and each player's payoff function is quasi-concave in their own strategy, a Nash equilibrium exists.

The proof uses the Kakutani fixed-point theorem on the best-response correspondence. Each player's best response to opponents' strategies forms a set; Kakutani guarantees a fixed point (mutual best response) exists if and only if these sets are convex-valued. Quasi-concavity of payoffs ensures that best-response sets are convex.

Without convexity, equilibrium may not exist. Consider a game where strategy spaces are non-convex (e.g., discrete choices with no intermediate options): best-response correspondences can cycle without fixed point. The existence of stable strategic equilibrium mathematically requires convex geometry.

This extends beyond economics. Any system of interacting agents pursuing optimization—biological fitness, neural activation, market prices—exhibits equilibrium only when the effective strategy spaces are convex or can be embedded in convex structures.

### 2.3 Optimal Transport Maps Are Gradients of Convex Functions

Brenier's theorem (1987) states: for the quadratic cost function c(x,y) = ||x-y||², the optimal transport map T sending distribution μ to distribution ν takes the form T = ∇φ for some convex function φ.

The optimal way to move mass from one distribution to another—minimizing total displacement cost—is to follow gradients of convex potentials. This is not merely convenient but mathematically necessary for quadratic costs.

McCann's displacement interpolation extends this: the entropy functional is convex along Wasserstein geodesics (optimal transport paths) if and only if the underlying space has non-negative Ricci curvature. This establishes a deep connection: convexity of entropy, geometry of space, and optimality of transport are mutually constraining.

The implication: any process optimizing resource allocation under energy costs—diffusion, heat flow, economic distribution, neural signal routing—follows convex gradients. Convexity is the geometric signature of optimal allocation.

### 2.4 Information Geometry Is Intrinsically Curved with Convex Structure

The probability simplex Δ^(n-1) = {p ∈ ℝⁿ : Σp_i = 1, p_i ≥ 0} carries a natural Riemannian metric: the Fisher information metric g_ij(θ) = E[∂log p/∂θ_i · ∂log p/∂θ_j].

Chentsov's theorem (1972) establishes that the Fisher metric is the unique Riemannian metric (up to scaling) invariant under sufficient statistics. There is no choice; any other metric would change under statistically irrelevant transformations.

Shannon entropy H(p) = -Σp_i log p_i is strictly concave: the Hessian ∂²H/∂p_i∂p_j = -δ_ij/p_i is negative definite everywhere in simplex interior. Maximum entropy occurs at the uniform distribution (simplex centroid).

The probability simplex itself is convex—any weighted combination of probability distributions remains a probability distribution. Information geometry thus exhibits convexity at multiple levels: the ambient space is convex, the entropy function is concave (convex in the Legendre-dual coordinates), and the natural metric respects this structure.

### 2.5 Synthesis: Convexity Is Not Observed But Required

The preceding arguments establish that convexity is not an empirical observation to be explained but a mathematical requirement for equilibrium existence:

1. **Thermodynamics**: Stability requires convex energy surfaces (non-convex systems fragment or explode)
2. **Game theory**: Equilibrium requires convex strategy spaces (Kakutani's theorem)
3. **Optimal transport**: Efficiency requires convex potentials (Brenier's theorem)
4. **Information geometry**: Probability structure is inherently convex (simplex geometry, concave entropy)

**The central claim follows: wherever the dual entropic drives (integrative and dispersive) reach stable balance, convex geometry necessarily emerges as the equilibrium signature.** This is not because nature "prefers" convexity but because non-convex equilibria are mathematically impossible.

## 3. The Holographic Principle and Geometric Information

The proposition that information is fundamental gains empirical precision through the holographic principle: the maximum information content of a spatial region scales with its boundary area, not its volume.

### 3.1 Bekenstein Bound and Black Hole Entropy

The Bekenstein bound states: the entropy S of any system is bounded by S ≤ 2πRE/ℏc, where R is circumscribing radius and E is total energy. For a black hole saturating this bound, S = A/4l_P² where A is horizon area and l_P is Planck length.

The Planck area l_P² = Gℏ/c³ ≈ 2.6×10⁻⁷⁰ m² represents the fundamental unit of information capacity: approximately one bit per Planck area. This is not a limit on our knowledge but on reality's storage capacity—a direct empirical consequence of information-as-fundamental.

The derivation proceeds through Hawking's generalized second law: when matter with entropy S falls into a black hole, total entropy (matter + horizon) must not decrease. This requires the horizon area to increase by at least 4l_P²·S, establishing the bit-per-Planck-area correspondence.

### 3.2 Ryu-Takayanagi Formula and Emergent Geometry

In the AdS/CFT correspondence, the Ryu-Takayanagi formula (2006) establishes: for a boundary region A, the entanglement entropy S_A equals the area of the minimal bulk surface γ_A bounded by ∂A, divided by 4G_N:

S_A = Area(γ_A)/4G_N

Entanglement entropy—a purely quantum-informational quantity—equals geometric surface area. Information IS geometry, stated mathematically.

The ER=EPR conjecture (Maldacena & Susskind, 2013) extends this: Einstein-Rosen bridges (wormholes) are equivalent to Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen correlations (entanglement). Two entangled black holes are connected by a wormhole; reducing entanglement lengthens the wormhole throat until spacetime disconnects. Van Raamsdonk's phrase captures the implication: "entanglement is the glue holding spacetime together."

### 3.3 Implications for Convexity

Convex polytopes have minimal surface area for given vertex configuration—they enclose maximum volume with minimum boundary. If information scales with boundary area (holographic principle), then convex structures maximize information capacity per unit boundary.

This connects the equilibrium argument to information storage: convex geometry emerges at equilibrium because it optimizes information integration per unit interface. The integrative drive (maximizing integrated information) naturally produces convex structures; the dispersive drive (maximizing entropy) constrains total capacity. Their balance yields convex equilibria.

- 2D: Infinitely many regular polygons
- 3D: 5 Platonic solids

Four dimensions is exceptional: it admits more regular forms than any other dimension. One of these, the 24-cell, exists only in 4D—it has no lower or higher-dimensional analog.

This is not arbitrary. The rotation group SO(4) has a unique structure: its Lie algebra decomposes as so(4) ≅ so(3) ⊕ so(3), making four-dimensional rotation equivalent to two independent three-dimensional rotations. This algebraic exceptionalism underlies the geometric exceptionalism.

### 4.2 Why the 24-Cell Specifically

The 24-cell satisfies a constellation of constraints that no other polytope matches:

**Self-duality**: The 24-cell's dual (formed by placing vertices at cell centers) is congruent to itself. Vertices and cells are geometrically interchangeable. This property is unique among non-simplex regular polytopes.

**Quaternionic structure**: The 24 vertices correspond exactly to the 24 unit Hurwitz quaternions: {±1, ±i, ±j, ±k} ∪ {½(±1±i±j±k)}. These form a group under quaternion multiplication—the binary tetrahedral group. The 24-cell embeds the algebra of rotations geometrically.

**Optimal sphere packing**: The D₄ lattice (vertices of the 24-cell repeated throughout 4D) achieves the densest sphere packing in four dimensions—kissing number 24. Each sphere touches exactly 24 neighbors. No arrangement permits more contact.

**Tripartite decomposition**: The 24 vertices partition exactly into three disjoint sets of 8, each forming a regular 16-cell. This partition is exhaustive (8+8+8=24) and unique. The index of the 16-cell symmetry group B₄ in the 24-cell symmetry group F₄ is exactly 3.

**Maximal symmetry per vertex**: The symmetry group F₄ has order 1,152—larger per vertex than any other regular 4-polytope except the 120-cell, which has 600 vertices (massively redundant).

No other polytope satisfies all these constraints. The tesseract lacks self-duality. The 16-cell lacks tripartite decomposition. The 600-cell has excess complexity without additional structure. The 24-cell occupies the unique position where self-reference, algebraic closure, optimal packing, and decomposability all coincide.

## 7. Empirical Predictions

A framework worth proposing must yield predictions distinguishing it from alternatives. The following are testable within the next decade:

If information gravitates (as the framework predicts), then gravitational effects should correlate with structural complexity, not merely mass. If vacuum energy is residual after structure formation has absorbed information capacity, then dark energy density should evolve over cosmic history—decreasing as structures formed, stabilizing as structure formation saturated.

**Test**: The equation-of-state parameter w should deviate from -1, with w > -1 in the matter-dominated era and approaching -1 as expansion accelerates. Recent DESI results showing hints of w ≠ -1 are consistent with this prediction.

**Falsification**: If precision measurements establish w = -1.000 ± 0.005 constant across all redshifts, the dynamic vacuum prediction fails.

### 7.3 Cross-Domain Transfer in Geometric Cognitive Systems

**Falsification**: If no transfer occurs between geometrically isomorphic domains despite structural correspondence, the universality claim fails.
